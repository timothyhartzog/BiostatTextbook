Chapter 6

Sampling Distributions & the Central Limit Theorem

Why can we make inferences about populations using small samples? The Central Limit Theorem is the key reason — explore it through simulation.

6.1 Sampling Distributions

When we draw many samples from a population and calculate a statistic (e.g., the mean) from each sample, the distribution of those statistics is called the sampling distribution of the statistic.

The sampling distribution of the sample mean x̄ has:

  • Mean = μ (population mean)
  • Standard deviation = σ / √n (called the Standard Error)
SE(x̄) = σ / √n

As n increases, SE decreases — larger samples give more precise estimates.

6.2 The Central Limit Theorem (CLT)

🎯 Central Limit Theorem If samples of size n are drawn from any population with mean μ and finite variance σ², then for large enough n the sampling distribution of x̄ is approximately normal, regardless of the shape of the population.

Rule of thumb: n ≥ 30 is usually sufficient; less is needed for symmetric populations.

The CLT is why the normal distribution is central to statistical inference — it justifies applying normal-based tests even when the population is not perfectly normal.

6.3 Confidence Intervals

A confidence interval (CI) is a range of values likely to contain the true population parameter. For the mean of a large sample:

95% CI: x̄ ± 1.96 × (σ / √n)

If σ is unknown (the usual case), use the sample s and t-distribution:

95% CI: x̄ ± t* × (s / √n)

where t* is the critical value from the t-distribution with n−1 degrees of freedom.

⚠️ Correct Interpretation A 95% CI does not mean "there's a 95% chance the true mean is in this interval." It means: if we repeated the study many times, 95% of the intervals constructed would contain the true mean.
