Chapter 4

Probability

Probability is the language of uncertainty. Mastering its basic rules is essential for understanding statistical inference.

4.1 Basic Definitions

The probability of an event A is a number between 0 and 1 representing how likely A is to occur:

0 ≤ P(A) ≤ 1
  • P(A) = 0 → impossible event
  • P(A) = 1 → certain event
  • P(A) + P(Aᶜ) = 1, where Aᶜ is the complement (A does not occur)

For equally likely outcomes:

P(A) = (number of outcomes in A) / (total outcomes in sample space)

4.2 Addition Rule

For any two events A and B:

P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B) − P(A ∩ B)

If A and B are mutually exclusive (cannot both occur):

P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B)
Example In a population, 30% have hypertension (H), 20% have diabetes (D), and 8% have both. What is the probability of having either condition?
P(H ∪ D) = 0.30 + 0.20 − 0.08 = 0.42

4.3 Multiplication Rule & Independence

Two events are independent if knowing one occurred gives no information about the other:

P(A ∩ B) = P(A) × P(B) [if A and B are independent]

In general:

P(A ∩ B) = P(A) × P(B | A)

where P(B | A) is the conditional probability of B given A.

4.4 Conditional Probability

The probability of B occurring given that A has already occurred:

P(B | A) = P(A ∩ B) / P(A), provided P(A) > 0
Example Among patients tested for COVID-19, 10% are positive. Of those who are positive, 90% have a fever. What is the probability a randomly chosen patient is both positive and has a fever?
P(Positive ∩ Fever) = P(Positive) × P(Fever | Positive) = 0.10 × 0.90 = 0.09

4.5 Bayes' Theorem

Bayes' theorem allows us to update a probability after observing new evidence:

P(A | B) = [P(B | A) × P(A)] / P(B)

This is fundamental to understanding diagnostic test performance.
