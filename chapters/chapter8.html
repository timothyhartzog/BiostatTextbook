Chapter 8

t-Tests

The t-test is the workhorse of biomedical research — used to compare means when the population standard deviation is unknown. Three variants: one-sample, two-sample, and paired.

8.1 One-Sample t-Test

Tests whether a sample mean differs from a known (hypothesised) population mean μ₀.

H₀: μ = μ₀   H₁: μ ≠ μ₀
t = (x̄ − μ₀) / (s / √n)   df = n − 1

Assumptions: Data approximately normally distributed (or n ≥ 30 by CLT), observations independent.

Example A sample of 25 adults has mean systolic BP = 126 mmHg and s = 12 mmHg. Is this significantly different from the normal value of 120 mmHg? t = (126 − 120) / (12/√25) = 6/2.4 = 2.50, df = 24.

8.2 Two-Sample t-Test (Welch)

Compares the means of two independent groups. Welch's version does not assume equal variances and is preferred by default.

t = (x̄₁ − x̄₂) / √(s₁²/n₁ + s₂²/n₂)
df ≈ (s₁²/n₁ + s₂²/n₂)² / [(s₁²/n₁)²/(n₁−1) + (s₂²/n₂)²/(n₂−1)] (Welch–Satterthwaite)

Assumptions: Independence of groups; approximately normal distributions or large n.

8.3 Paired t-Test

Used when measurements come in natural pairs (e.g., before/after, matched subjects). Calculate the difference d = x₁ − x₂ for each pair, then apply a one-sample t-test on d.

t = d̄ / (sₐ / √n)   df = n − 1

The paired design removes between-subject variability and is generally more powerful than an independent two-sample test when pairing is appropriate.

8.5 Choosing the Right t-Test

SituationTest
One sample vs known valueOne-sample t-test
Two independent groupsIndependent (Welch) t-test
Before and after (same subjects)Paired t-test
