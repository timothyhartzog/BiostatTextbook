Chapter 5

Probability Distributions

A probability distribution describes all possible values a random variable can take and how likely each is. Interactive visualisers let you explore distributions in real time.

5.1 The Normal Distribution

The normal (Gaussian) distribution is the most important in biostatistics. It is bell-shaped, symmetric about its mean, and fully characterised by two parameters:

X ~ N(μ, σ²)
  • μ = mean (location)
  • σ = standard deviation (spread)

The probability density function (PDF):

f(x) = (1 / (σ√(2π))) × e^(−½((x−μ)/σ)²)

The 68–95–99.7 Rule

  • ~68% of values fall within ±1σ of the mean
  • ~95% of values fall within ±2σ of the mean
  • ~99.7% of values fall within ±3σ of the mean

Standard Normal (Z) Distribution

Any normal distribution can be standardised:

Z = (X − μ) / σ, where Z ~ N(0, 1)

5.3 The Binomial Distribution

The binomial distribution models the number of successes in n independent trials, each with probability p of success:

X ~ Binomial(n, p)
P(X = k) = C(n,k) × p^k × (1−p)^(n−k)
  • Mean: μ = np
  • Variance: σ² = np(1−p)
Example If 20% of patients experience a side effect and we treat 10 patients, what is the probability exactly 2 experience the side effect? P(X=2) with n=10, p=0.20.

5.5 The Poisson Distribution

The Poisson distribution models counts of rare events occurring in a fixed time or space interval:

X ~ Poisson(λ)
P(X = k) = (e^−λ × λ^k) / k!

Mean = Variance = λ. Used for hospital admission rates, mutation counts, etc.
