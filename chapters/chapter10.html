Chapter 10

Correlation & Simple Linear Regression

Explore linear relationships between two continuous variables: measure their strength with correlation, and model them with regression.

10.1 Pearson Correlation Coefficient (r)

Pearson's r quantifies the strength and direction of the linear relationship between two continuous variables.

r = Σ[(xᵢ−x̄)(yᵢ−ȳ)] / √[Σ(xᵢ−x̄)² × Σ(yᵢ−ȳ)²]
  • r = +1: Perfect positive linear relationship
  • r = 0: No linear relationship
  • r = −1: Perfect negative linear relationship
|r| valueInterpretation
0.00 – 0.10Negligible
0.10 – 0.30Weak
0.30 – 0.50Moderate
0.50 – 0.70Strong
0.70 – 1.00Very strong
⚠️ Correlation ≠ Causation A strong correlation does not imply that X causes Y. A third (confounding) variable may explain both.

10.2 Simple Linear Regression

Regression models the relationship between a predictor (X) and a continuous outcome (Y):

Ŷ = b₀ + b₁X
  • b₀ (intercept): expected Y when X = 0
  • b₁ (slope): change in Y for a one-unit increase in X

Estimating the Slope and Intercept (Least Squares)

b₁ = Σ[(xᵢ−x̄)(yᵢ−ȳ)] / Σ(xᵢ−x̄)²
b₀ = ȳ − b₁x̄

Goodness of Fit: R²

R² (coefficient of determination) is the proportion of variance in Y explained by X:

R² = 1 − (SSresidual / SStotal)

R² = r² for simple linear regression. R² = 0.7 means the model explains 70% of variance in Y.

10.4 Assumptions of Linear Regression

  • Linearity: The relationship between X and Y is linear.
  • Independence: Observations are independent.
  • Homoscedasticity: Residuals have constant variance across all fitted values.
  • Normality of residuals: Residuals are approximately normally distributed (important for inference in small samples).
  • No influential outliers that excessively distort the line.
