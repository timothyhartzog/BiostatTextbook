Chapter 2

Types of Variables

Variables are the measurements we collect. Understanding what kind of variable you have is the first step in choosing the right statistical method.

2.1 The Measurement Hierarchy

Stevens (1946) proposed four levels of measurement, each building on the previous:

Nominal

Categories with no order. Labels only. Example: blood type (A, B, AB, O), sex.

Ordinal

Categories with a meaningful order, but intervals between levels are not equal. Example: cancer stage (I, II, III, IV), pain scale (1–10).

Interval

Ordered with equal intervals, but no true zero. Example: temperature (°C), calendar year.

Ratio

Equal intervals plus a true zero. Example: weight, height, blood glucose, age, counts.

💡 Why Does This Matter? Only ratio/interval data can meaningfully use arithmetic (means, standard deviations). Nominal/ordinal data require non-parametric methods or special models.

2.2 Quantitative vs Qualitative Variables

A broader classification divides variables into two groups:

TypeDescriptionExamplesMeasurement scale
Quantitative (Numerical)Numbers that represent quantitiesBlood pressure, age, BMIInterval or Ratio
Qualitative (Categorical)Groups or categoriesDiagnosis, sex, ethnicityNominal or Ordinal

2.3 Discrete vs Continuous Variables

Quantitative variables can be further split:

  • Discrete: Countable values, often integers. Examples: number of hospital visits, number of children, white blood cell count.
  • Continuous: Can take any value in a range. Examples: height, body temperature, serum cholesterol.
⚠️ Practical Note In practice, continuous variables are always rounded to some precision. A variable measured on a fine enough scale (e.g., glucose with many decimal places) is treated as continuous.

2.4 Binary and Dichotomous Variables

A special case of nominal variable with only two categories (e.g., disease present/absent, alive/dead). These are extremely common in biomedical research and are handled with logistic regression and risk ratios.
