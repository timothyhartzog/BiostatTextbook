Chapter 1

Introduction to Biostatistics

Understanding what biostatistics is, why it matters, and the key concepts that underpin all statistical thinking in health and life sciences.

1.1 What Is Biostatistics?

Biostatistics is the application of statistical methods to biological and health-related data. It provides the mathematical tools to design studies, collect data, and draw valid conclusions about populations from limited samples.

Without biostatistics, we could not:

  • Determine whether a new drug is more effective than a placebo.
  • Identify risk factors for a disease in an epidemiological study.
  • Set safe exposure limits for environmental toxins.
  • Evaluate the accuracy of a diagnostic test.
💡 Key Point Biostatistics bridges the gap between raw data and meaningful conclusions. It quantifies uncertainty so we can make decisions with known confidence.

1.2 Populations and Samples

A population is the complete set of individuals or measurements we want to draw conclusions about. A sample is a subset of that population that we actually study.

ConceptDefinitionExample
PopulationAll individuals of interestAll adults aged 40–65 in the USA
SampleMeasured subset of the population2,000 adults randomly selected from 50 states
ParameterNumerical summary of a populationTrue mean systolic blood pressure (μ)
StatisticNumerical summary of a sampleSample mean blood pressure (x̄)

Because measuring entire populations is usually impossible, we use statistics to estimate parameters — and we quantify how uncertain those estimates are.

1.3 Types of Studies

The type of study design determines what statistical conclusions are valid.

Observational Studies

The researcher observes without intervening. These can establish associations but generally not causation.

  • Cross-sectional: Snapshot at one time point (e.g., prevalence surveys).
  • Case-control: Compare people with a disease (cases) to those without (controls), looking back at exposures.
  • Cohort: Follow a group over time to see who develops an outcome.

Experimental Studies

The researcher assigns the intervention. When randomised, these can establish causation.

  • Randomised Controlled Trial (RCT): Participants randomly assigned to treatment or control. The gold standard for evaluating interventions.
  • Crossover trial: Each participant receives both treatment and control at different times.
⚠️ Correlation ≠ Causation Observational studies can only show associations. Confounding variables may explain apparent relationships. Only a well-designed experiment with randomisation can establish causality.

1.4 The Statistical Analysis Process

A biostatistical analysis follows a structured workflow:

  1. Define the research question and identify the population of interest.
  2. Choose the study design appropriate for the question.
  3. Collect data using a reproducible, unbiased protocol.
  4. Explore and clean the data (descriptive statistics, checking for errors).
  5. Choose the appropriate statistical test based on data type and study design.
  6. Interpret results in the context of the original question.
  7. Communicate findings with appropriate uncertainty statements.
