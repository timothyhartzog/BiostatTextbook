Chapter 3

Descriptive Statistics

Summarise and explore data using measures of centre, spread, and shape before applying any formal tests.

3.1 Measures of Central Tendency

These statistics describe where the "middle" of a dataset lies.

Mean

The arithmetic average. Sum all values and divide by the number of observations.

x̄ = (Σ xᵢ) / n

The mean uses every value, making it sensitive to outliers. Best for symmetric, continuous data.

Median

The middle value when data are sorted. Robust to outliers. Preferred for skewed data (e.g., income, hospital length of stay).

Mode

The most frequently occurring value. Useful for categorical data; a dataset can be unimodal, bimodal, or multimodal.

💡 Choosing the Right Measure Use the mean for symmetric distributions; use the median for skewed distributions or when outliers are present.

3.2 Measures of Spread (Variability)

Range

Maximum − Minimum. Simple but sensitive to extremes.

Interquartile Range (IQR)

The distance between the 25th percentile (Q1) and the 75th percentile (Q3). Contains the middle 50% of data and is robust to outliers.

IQR = Q3 − Q1

Variance (s²)

Average squared deviation from the mean (using n − 1 for a sample).

s² = Σ(xᵢ − x̄)² / (n − 1)

Standard Deviation (s)

Square root of the variance; expressed in the same units as the data. The most commonly used measure of spread for normally distributed data.

s = √[Σ(xᵢ − x̄)² / (n − 1)]

Standard Error of the Mean (SEM)

How much the sample mean is expected to vary from sample to sample.

SEM = s / √n

3.3 Shape: Skewness and Kurtosis

Skewness measures asymmetry:

  • Right (positive) skew: Long tail to the right; mean > median. Common for income, hospital stays.
  • Left (negative) skew: Long tail to the left; mean < median.
  • Symmetric: Mean ≈ median (e.g., normal distribution).

Kurtosis measures the heaviness of tails relative to a normal distribution. High kurtosis (leptokurtic) means more extreme outliers.
